🕯️ Candlestick Charts

A candlestick shows 4 numbers for each time period: where price opened, the highest it went, the lowest it went, and where it closed.

Think of it like a report card for each hour or day. The fat body shows where it started vs ended. The thin lines (wicks) show how wild the ride was.

Green candle = price went UP (closed higher than it opened). Bulls won that round.

Red candle = price went DOWN (closed lower). Bears won that round.

Long upper wick = buyers tried to push it higher but sellers slapped it back down. Bearish sign.

Long lower wick = sellers tried to crash it but buyers stepped in and saved it. Bullish sign.

Tiny body (Doji) = nobody won. Price opened and closed at nearly the same level. Big move coming — watch the next candle.

📊 Chart Types

Candlestick — The trader's favorite. Shows the full story of each period. Best for spotting patterns and timing entries/exits.

Line Chart — Connects only closing prices. Strips away the noise. Great for seeing the big trend without getting distracted.

Like looking at a mountain range from far away vs up close — sometimes the overview tells you more.

OHLC Bars — Same data as candles but shown as bars. Left tick = open, right tick = close. Old-school traders love this.

Area Chart — A filled line chart. The shaded area makes trends pop visually. Good for portfolio dashboards.

📐 Moving Averages

SMA (Simple Moving Average) — The average closing price over the last N days. Smooths out the noise so you can see the real trend.

Like averaging your last 20 test scores to see your actual grade trend — one bad day doesn't tank the average.

EMA (Exponential Moving Average) — Like the SMA but recent prices count more. Reacts faster to new moves. The 9 and 21 EMA are popular for short-term trading.

When price is above a moving average → bullish trend. When it's below → bearish trend. When the short MA crosses above the long MA — that's a Golden Cross, one of the most famous bullish signals.

⚡ Bollinger Bands & RSI

Bollinger Bands — Three lines around price: a middle average, and upper/lower bands 2 standard deviations away. When the bands squeeze (get narrow), a big move is about to happen. When price touches the upper band, it may be overbought.

Imagine a rubber band being stretched — the further it stretches, the more likely it snaps back.

RSI (Relative Strength Index) — A number from 0–100 that measures momentum. Above 70 = overbought (price may drop). Below 30 = oversold (price may bounce). The most widely used indicator in the world.

💹 Asset Types Explained

Stocks — Tiny ownership slices of a company. If Apple makes more money, your Apple stock is worth more. You share in the company's wins and losses.

Like buying a small piece of a pizza restaurant. If business booms, your slice of the business is worth more.

Crypto — Digital currencies that live on blockchains. No central authority controls them. Extremely volatile — prices can move 20% in a day. Bitcoin and Ethereum are the two most established.

ETF (Exchange Traded Fund) — A basket of many stocks or assets bundled into one thing you can trade like a stock. SPY holds all 500 S&P 500 companies in one share.

Like buying a variety pack instead of picking just one flavor — instant diversification.

🏦 Index Funds vs ETFs

People confuse these all the time. Here's the real difference:

ETF — Trades live on the stock exchange all day, every second. Price changes constantly just like a stock. You buy at whatever price it is right now.

Index Fund (Mutual Fund) — Only prices once per day at market close. You don't know your exact buy price until after the market closes. Usually has a minimum investment. Buffett famously recommends these for most people.

ETF = buying oranges at the grocery store (price shown right now). Index Fund = ordering oranges for delivery — you get tomorrow's price, whatever it ends up being.

Both track the same indexes. Both are great for long-term investing. ETFs are just more flexible to trade.

🥇 Commodities

Commodities are physical goods that are traded in bulk — things like gold, oil, wheat, and coffee. Prices are set by global supply and demand.

If a drought destroys wheat crops in Russia, wheat prices spike worldwide. That's commodity trading.

Gold — Called a "safe haven." When stock markets crash, people flee to gold. It holds value over centuries. Central banks hold tons of it.

Oil (WTI / Brent) — The most traded commodity in the world. Every economy runs on oil, so its price affects everything — airlines, shipping, plastics.

Agricultural — Wheat, corn, soybeans, coffee, sugar. Affected by weather, seasons, and geopolitics.

💱 Forex (Foreign Exchange)

Forex is the buying and selling of currencies. EUR/USD means "how many US dollars does one Euro buy?" If EUR/USD goes from 1.08 to 1.09, the Euro got stronger against the Dollar.

If you travel to Europe and exchange dollars for Euros, you're doing forex. Traders just do it on a massive scale to profit from rate changes.

Major pairs — EUR/USD, GBP/USD, USD/JPY. Most traded, most liquid, tightest spreads.

Exotic pairs — USD/TRY (Turkish Lira), USD/ZAR (South African Rand). More volatile, bigger moves, more risky.

Forex trades 24/5 — the only market open all night (it closes on weekends).

🌍 Market Indices

An index tracks a group of stocks to represent the overall health of a market. You can't buy an index directly — it's just a number.

S&P 500 — The 500 largest US companies. When people say "the market is up today," they usually mean this. It's the most watched number in finance.

Dow Jones — Just 30 huge companies like Apple, Boeing, Goldman Sachs. Old and famous but less representative than the S&P 500.

VIX — The "Fear Index." Measures expected volatility. When VIX spikes above 30, markets are panicking. Below 15 = calm markets.

Think of the S&P 500 like the GPA of the US economy — it averages out all the good and bad to give one number.

📋 Order Types

Market Order — "Buy/sell RIGHT NOW at whatever price exists." Guaranteed to fill instantly but you get whatever price the market gives you.

Like waving down a taxi and saying "just go" — you'll get there but you're at their mercy on the route and price.

Limit Order — "Only buy if the price drops to X" or "only sell if it rises to Y." You control the price but it might never fill.

Like telling the taxi "I'll only pay $20 max." You might wait a long time for the right driver.

Stop-Loss — An automatic sell if price falls below a certain level. Protects you from catastrophic losses while you sleep. Every professional uses these.

Like setting an alarm so you wake up before you're late — the stop-loss wakes up your risk management.

💰 Key Portfolio Terms

P&L (Profit & Loss) — How much you've made or lost. "Unrealized" P&L is gains on positions you still hold. "Realized" P&L is what you've actually locked in by selling.

Cost Basis / Avg Cost — The average price you paid across all your buys. If you bought 10 shares at $100 and 10 more at $120, your cost basis is $110.

P/E Ratio — Price-to-Earnings. How much you pay for each $1 of profit. A P/E of 25 means you're paying $25 for every $1 the company earns per year. High P/E = expensive but high growth expected. Low P/E = cheap or slow-growing.

Like paying $25 for a pizza shop that earns $1/year. You'd need 25 years to break even — that's a high P/E.

Market Cap — Total value of all shares. Share price × total shares. Apple at $3T market cap is worth more than most countries' GDPs.

Volume — How many shares traded today. High volume on a green day = strong buying conviction. Low volume moves are often fake-outs that reverse.

⚠️ Leveraged ETFs (Be Careful!)

Leveraged ETFs like TQQQ (3x Nasdaq) amplify daily moves by 2x or 3x. If the Nasdaq goes up 1%, TQQQ goes up 3%. If it drops 1%, TQQQ drops 3%.

Like betting $300 instead of $100. Wins are huge. Losses can wipe you out in days.

Inverse ETFs like SQQQ go UP when the market goes DOWN. Used to hedge or bet on crashes.

⚠️ Warning: Leveraged ETFs decay over time due to daily rebalancing. They are NOT for long-term holding. They're short-term trading tools only.
